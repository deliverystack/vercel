Then I got too lazy to analyze it and asked google's gemini "AI" to summarize that transcript in 10 bullets. I wouldn't mind reviewing the video content again, but I've already assimilated some and I have other things to do. Wow, these "AI" tools are useful, but I don't see them being highly profitable.

  • Mass Amnesia: A "slow fade" and "blur" of memory is creating a quiet, unnoticed crisis of mass amnesia, making people feel foggy, off, unfocused, and not quite themselves.
  • Algorithms Curate Reality: Algorithms manipulate what you see, feel, and crave (anger, attention, loneliness), effectively building who you become rather than reflecting who you are.
  • Structural Erosion of Self: This algorithmic curation is a "structural erosion of self" and identity, where instincts dull and beliefs soften, leading people to be "led somewhere" by manufactured reality.
  • Overstimulated Nervous System: Constant scrolling, swiping, and artificial inputs overstimulate the nervous system, resulting in feeling numb, constantly reactive, and swinging between apathy and agitation.
  • Breakdown in Memory Encoding: Modern life prevents deep, undistracted focus, which is essential for transferring short-term experiences into long-term memory, leading to a breakdown where experiences are "written in disappearing ink."
  • Cognitive Drift and Loss of Self: Attention fragments, a state called cognitive drift, which in this era becomes something darker--forgetting not just moments, but your core identity, passions, and sense of meaning.
  • The Blueprint: Fracture Identity for Control: The ultimate goal is to fracture memory and identity, so you willingly control yourself and "buy any story they give you," thinking the numbness and silence are your own ideas.

The last three bullets are about six steps for recovery, or finding the way back, so I followed up with the AI by asking it to list those.

  1. See the Fog: Start tracking every reach for your phone and every scroll. Don't fight the trance; just notice the trance and become aware of it. The fog loses power when it is seen.
  2. Drop the Mask Completely: Refuse to be obsessed with how people perceive you. Say three true things that you have never said before, even to yourself.
  3. Get Into Your Body: Return to the instrument of your being. Get mindful, feel your ribs move, and breathe like it's your only job.
  4. Return to and Recover What You Buried: Recover a dream, a passion, or some "wildness" that was exiled but not extinguished from inside you.
  5. Make the Ordinary Things Sacred: Your words shape the future. This practice is about using neuroplasticity to get your brain back by assigning significance to everyday actions and words.
  6. Leave a Trail: Write down one sentence (e.g., "I was here and I remembered") and tape it anywhere you are going to see it to constantly remind yourself and assert your memory.

In contemplation on re-evaluating my core values, I came across Schwartz's theory of basic human values:

  • https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_basic_human_values

This defines ten types of values:

  • Self-Direction: Freedom, creativity, independent thought. These would be some of my primary values.
  • Stimulation: Novelty, excitement, challenge. Boredom, or time without stimulation, is also critical.
  • Hedonism: Pleasure, enjoyment of life. Seeking pleasure can be dangerous and must be balanced against others.
  • Achievement: Success, demonstrating competence. I prefer to have goals than accomplishments, but both can enhance feelings of self-worth.
  • Power: Status, control over resources and others. I have lost interest in status and hope that I do not want to control resources or others.
  • Security: Safety, stability, order. In this context I seem to prefer chaos and insecurity.
  • Conformity: Obedience, self-discipline, social rules. Other than self-discipline, hell no.
  • Tradition: Respect for customs, cultural or religious norms. These all require individual evaluation, but I try to respect others.
  • Benevolence: Care for close others, loyalty, helping. I am re-evaluating these, where I have focused in the past.
  • Universalism: Concern for all people and the planet: fairness, tolerance, environmentalism. Virtues, compassion for the planet, and reducing suffering for all sentient beings are important to me.

These ten values reflect two major tensions: Openness to Change vs Conservation (autonomy vs. maintaining the status quo) and Self-Enhancement vs Self-Transcendence (pursuing personal gain vs. caring for others.).

  • Openness: self-direction, stimulation, (hedonism partly).
  • Conservation: security, conformity, tradition.
  • Self-enhancement: power, achievement (hedonism partly).
  • Self-transcendence: benevolence, universalism.

The values can be arranged in a circle, where adjacent values are compatible but opposite values conflict.
